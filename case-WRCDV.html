What is Women’s Resource Center?

Women’s Resource Center to End Domestic Violence has been helping women and their families escape DV for over 20 years. They are a local non-profit who’s had a big impact on the community. They provide safety, legal, and lifestyle help to families to transition into healthy, safe environments.

Who are we trying to impact?

Our persona Sabrina, represents their audience. A 26-year-old, college educated - go-getter with a passion for helping people. Helping her roommate get out of a recent domestic violence situation sparked her curiousity in how she might be able to have more of an impact locally and politically.

What was the business challenge?

WRCDV’s intention was to broaden their reach to millennials, and redesign their website to make it more engaging and user friendly.

Research Highlights

Branding - Color/image treatment

Current logo and brand colors could not be changed per the stakeholder’s request.

  • Con - Images on the site were low quality and weren’t consistent with the polished brand look & feel.
  • Pro - We can measure image & color treatment through interviews, and usability testing.
  • Pro - Colors were carefully chosen to project empowerment, transformation, and self-value.

Long-term vision & initiative

WRCDV wishes to broaden their reach to engage with millennials & Gen Z’s.
Based on the vision we can create strategies to increase communication to the target audience through their respective mediums - Facebook, Instagram, twitter, etc.
Focusing on this target group will allow us to recruit them as key resources (Business Model Canvas).

Current Site Traffic

We discovered that Donors have a hard time finding the info they seek. This could be a potential opportunity to help WRC increase their revenue stream.

  • 30% of their traffic is a result of donors visiting the site
  • 75% of which are women between the ages of 40 and 60
  • 50% of the site’s content is geared towards donors

Insights from the stakeholders

We conducted stakeholder interviews with the Director of Development, Amber Harris, and the Director of Operations, Markesha Dunham. My team and I quickly debriefed after each session. Our goal was to better understand why millennials & gen z users were not engaging with the WRCDV website. The following pain points were highlighted from these two interviews:

  • Lack of mobile responsiveness
  • Very little content to draw in potential volunteer interaction
  • Site organization was creating headaches for visitors trying to donate money or goods to the center
  • A desire to update the site with more gender inclusive language.

Stakeholders

How the current site caters to our Persona?

We identified two personas, the volunteer, and the patron. With the interest of time and aligning with the business goal of expanding their reach to millennials we chose to build on the volunteer persona. The current site currently has very little information tailored to volunteers. In developing the persona, we took into account that the site's visitors, employees, donors, and volunteers are overwhelmingly female.

Phase III

DEVELOP

Determining Information Architecture

The navigation for the site was convoluted with information. Our initial research also told us that Donors had difficulty finding information on the site. To tackle this we took a first pass at card sorting information on the site as a team and then tested peole who represented our target audience.

Card Sorting

Conducting 3 separate card sorting sessions allowed us to get an idea of how our audience would potentially organize the information on the site. We analyzed the results together with our insight & prioritized features to create a navigation that would potentially be better than its predecessor.

Prioritizing the Site Map

The original site map had seven categories, some extremely vague, requiring visitors to hover over them to see what they contained. We opted for a much simpler navigation that addressed the site’s 3 main visitor types. The Women’s Resource Center’s focus is helping victims of domestic violence, so our first category is Get Help. Under this falls all the ways a victim can get help in a violent situation. Our second category is How to Help.

Sixty percent of site visitors are either volunteers or donors to the center. With that in mind, we have one category that addresses everything both groups might need to know, and at the far right of the site’s top navigation bar, we’ve created two bright buttons addressing their needs individually, Volunteer and Donate. Our third and final category is About Us. Here, we placed the center’s mission, partners and affiliate sites, staff, annual reports and other information.

User Flow

Volunteer user flow

WRC requested that we enable the site to host an online volunteer request form. They were being inundated with phone calls from people needing additional information that was not available on the website. As an output from our Brainstorming methods we thought that volunteers like our persona, Sabrina, would find it easier to see upcoming opportunities, as well as, the roles available for her.

What if Volunteers already in the system?

One trouble spot we had to work out, was ensuring that first time volunteers didn’t sign up for an upcoming event without having filled out a volunteer application and passed the mandatory background check. We solved this using a simple popup window that asks if the user has ever volunteered with the center or its affiliates before. If they answered no, it takes the user to the volunteer form first and then pushes them to the event sign up form. WRC would receive both their volunteer and event sign up forms at the same time. Wireframes

My Team and I each created individual wireframes, then reviewed them together to ensure we were best incorporating the features we prioritized. It was challenging for us to come to a consensus on the arrangement of the elements in the header.

For example the location of volunteer and Donation buttons are typically placed in the upper right hand corner of headers - we agreed on following this convention. We also decided to place the crisis hotline being on a banner above the header. As we agreed on different points, we live sketched what it would look like.

Paper Wireframes

Usability Testing

My team and I wanted to test the overall ease of use and functionality of registering and picking a volunteer opportunity. We broke this down into modular pieces so we could garner acute feedback.

  • Find & fill out a 1st time volunteer form
  • Find & distinguish specific volunteer opportunities
  • Sign up for an event

Setup and capturing information

Idealy we would always have atleast 2 people conducting each usability test; one person interacting with the participant and another to record observations to make sure nuances aren't missed. To ensure the information captured were consistent, we created a test plan that outlined the following for each task:

  • Goal/Output
  • Assumptions
  • Steps
  • Success Criteria
  • Notes

Usability Test Results

75% used the volunteer button

Through our observations we saw that 75% of users test chose use the volunteer button to get to the Volunteer page instead of finding it through the navigation drop down.

Forms are easy to fill out

Users found the "New Volunteer Request Form" easy and straightforward.

Confusing layout elements

We discovered that our users weren’t able to identify “Volunteer Opportunities” and didn’t really know what “Opportunities” meant (our original heading). Testing allowed us to refine our copy, as we saw how creating better hierarchy in the copy works together with the alignment of relative UI elements to help users to achieve their intended goal. The bubbles also felt more like graphic elements than clickable items.
