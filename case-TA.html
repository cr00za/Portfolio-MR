The problem space

Planning international trips are never easy, especially when there are barriers such as unknown cultural customs, not being able to communicate because of language differences, being unfamiliar with medical and visa requirements, and navigating foreign environments.

How data helped identify the problem?

I surveyed 25 respondents who traveled internationally to determine what were their biggest concerns and challenges involved with the process of traveling. About 90% of them said they've forgotten to take something on their trip. This prompted me to focus on forgetting critical items that may prevent you from traveling, as well as, items you may need to pack.

Anticipated business challenges?

The ability to access travel data from other systems in order to allow Travel Flow to be a central place to manage travel itineraries that wouldn't require heavy emphasis on backend development.

Research Highlights

Forgetting items

89.5% of respondents admitted that they've forgotten to bring something on a trip.

Budgeting

58% of respondents said that budgeting was a challenging part of planning a trip.

Advice

60% found that reviews and advice from a friend helped them make decisions.

How would Travel Flow compete?

I conducted a formal competitive analysis using SWOT for each competitor (Strengths, Weaknesses, Threats, and Opportunities). Based on this I came up with the following ways Travel Flow could enter this market space and set itself apart from the rest:

  • Offline access to info
  • Add trip details on to existing Google, Apple calendars
  • Weather updates for destination
  • To do list/reminders for pack essential travel items
  • personable tone of copy
  • Recruit Travel Ambassadors for Social Media campaigns
  • Easy price structure

Persona

Using characteristics of the ideal user and insights that bridged my survey, interviews, and affinity diagram themes, I created a persona, Zayah, who I could always refer back to remind me of who I'm designing for.

Zayah is an experienced traveler who appreciates organization and gets frustrated when she can't access wifi when she needs it.

Feature Prioritization

Using User Insight to define the problem

I used the quantitative insights from my survey data, as well as, the qualitative insights from my interviews to generate a focused problem statement.

How might we create a way to manage trip details in a simple, clear, and easy manner so that preparation tasks are not tedious?

Prioritizing features based on need and feasibility

Typically, for an MVP, I wouldn't seek to pull info from other booking services, but I decided to explore what this would look like interactively. The following features were generated as a result:

  • Alert for req. documents i.e. visa, passport.
  • Travel itinerary
  • Weather
  • Packing list
  • Pull info from booking services

Crafting a story to generate opportunities

By creating a travel story to Australia and using Scenario, Journey mapping, and Storyboarding opportunities revealed themselves both for the user and the business. Some of these included developing local business partnerships during the travel phase, using a personable tone for reminders, and providing on-boarding vids to prepare travelers for their upcoming trip.

Storyboarding - Scenario - Journey mapping

Each helped me to view the phases of traveling to Australia from a slightly different angle. In the future I'd look to analyze and layout a story using a combination of all three of these methods. Most of the insight was gained from Journey Mapping, then Scenario, and finally Storyboarding.

Paper Wireframe

Layout and access for each prioritized feature

I considered what was most practical for accessing and placement of the primary features. I was able to validate most my assumptions by observing any difficulties, hesitations, or frustrations that the users experienced during testing and by asking for feedback. One such item was when to show the user their trip itinerary - was it a good idea to present a list of upcoming trips to the customer first or the actual itinerary of the next upcoming trip?

Features I focused on:

  • Sign up, Login, on-boarding, and consolidating travel information
  • Regulatory trip requirements
  • Personal packing List with choice of Avatar
  • Trip tips & suggestions
  • New trip confirmation
  • Pull info from booking services - MVP: manually enter information

Digital Wireframing and Usability Testing

The first digital wireframe iteration was designed for the iphone 6. Feedback from usability testing revealed that I should be designing for a more updated phone model. I decided to go with the iphone X because of it was currently trending and its relatively universal screen size. While it provided more screen landscape to work with, I had to be cognizant of the notch at the top of this model.

Updated items based on feedback and iOS guidelines

  • Used solid borders instead of dashed for itinerary - due to its affordance of something being editable
  • Return to travel dashboard made easier - via tab bar
  • Confusing verbiage for confirming that a passport has been renewed
  • Main features accessible through tab bar
  • Border radius - iOS standard 14px/pts
  • Font family - iOS standard SF Pro Text
